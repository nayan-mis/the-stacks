# Reading guide — psychology, UI craft, and design identity

Curated for two goals: make logging/tracking feel effortless, and make the product itself feel fast, smooth, and unmistakably its own.

## Core behavior-design theory
- **Tiny Habits** — BJ Fogg (2019). The Fogg Behavior Model: Behavior = Motivation × Ability × Prompt. Motivation is unreliable — design for Ability instead. *Applied:* logging a metric should take under 5 seconds. Auto-capture and one-tap entries beat any form.
- **Hooked** — Nir Eyal (2014). The Hook Model: Trigger → Action → Variable Reward → Investment. *Applied with a caveat:* lean on the "Investment" loop (accumulated data becomes genuinely more valuable over time) rather than exploitative variable rewards. Retention should come from real utility, not compulsion loops.
- **Drive** — Daniel Pink (2009), popularizing Self-Determination Theory (Deci & Ryan). Autonomy, competence, and relatedness drive intrinsic motivation better than external rewards. *Applied:* let users choose what/how to track (autonomy); show visible progress and mastery (competence).
- **Atomic Habits** — James Clear (2018). Habit stacking, identity-based habits, systems over goals. *Applied:* onboarding should anchor tracking to a habit the user already has ("after I open my laptop, check the dashboard").

## Cognitive load & usability
- **The Design of Everyday Things** — Don Norman (1988/2013). Affordances, signifiers, feedback, mapping — the foundation under every UI decision.
- **Don't Make Me Think** — Steve Krug (2000/2014). Every extra decision is friction; navigation should be self-evident.
- **Thinking, Fast and Slow** — Daniel Kahneman (2011). System 1 vs. System 2, the peak-end rule, default effects, anchoring. *Applied:* the moment a dashboard updates should feel like a small reward (peak); sessions should end cleanly, not abruptly.
- **Nudge** — Thaler & Sunstein (2008/2021). Choice architecture, smart defaults. *Applied:* pre-select sensible metrics for new users instead of a blank slate.

## Applied UI psychology
- **Seductive Interaction Design** — Stephen Anderson (2011). Maps psychology directly onto interface patterns.
- **Emotional Design** — Don Norman (2004). Visceral, behavioral, and reflective levels of design — why some tools feel good to use beyond pure function.

## Research areas (not books)
- Quantified Self movement — attrition studies on why most self-trackers quit within weeks. Directly relevant: study the drop-off point, design against it.
- Nielsen Norman Group — published UX research on habit/fitness-tracker interaction patterns.
- Zeigarnik effect — incomplete tasks stick in memory; a "3 of 5 connected" progress indicator nudges completion.
- IKEA effect — people value what they helped build; light dashboard customization increases attachment more than a fixed template.

## Primary research papers
The actual papers behind the concepts above, if you want the primary source instead of a summary:
- Fogg, B.J. (2009). "A Behavior Model for Persuasive Design." *Persuasive '09*, Article 40. The original B=MAP paper.
- Miller, R.B. (1968). "Response Time in Man-Computer Conversational Transactions." *AFIPS Fall Joint Computer Conference*. Source of the 0.1s/1s/10s thresholds.
- Kahneman, D., Fredrickson, B.L., Schreiber, C.A., & Redelmeier, D.A. (1993). "When More Pain Is Preferred to Less: Adding a Better End." *Psychological Science*, 4(6). The experiment behind the peak-end rule.
- Zeigarnik, B. (1927). "Über das Behalten von erledigten und unerledigten Handlungen." *Psychologische Forschung*. The original (German) Zeigarnik effect study — usually easier to access via English secondary summaries.
- Norton, M.I., Mochon, D., & Ariely, D. (2012). "The IKEA Effect: When Labor Leads to Love." *Journal of Consumer Psychology*, 22(3).
- Deci, E.L., & Ryan, R.M. (2000). "The 'What' and 'Why' of Goal Pursuits: Human Needs and the Self-Determination of Behavior." *Psychological Inquiry*, 11(4). Self-Determination Theory, formal version.
- Sweller, J. (1988). "Cognitive Load During Problem Solving: Effects on Learning." *Cognitive Science*, 12(2). Formal basis for "every extra field taxes working memory."
- Clawson, J., Pater, J.A., Miller, A.D., Mynatt, E.D., & Mamykina, L. (2015). "No Longer Wearing: Investigating the Abandonment of Personal Health-Tracking Technologies on Craigslist." *UbiComp '15*, 647–658. Real data on why self-trackers actually quit — the single most directly relevant paper to this product.
- Cleveland, W.S., & McGill, R. (1984). "Graphical Perception: Theory, Experimentation, and Application to the Development of Graphical Methods." *Journal of the American Statistical Association*, 79(387), 531–554. The empirical basis for "bar charts beat pie charts" — position and length judgments are measurably more accurate than angle judgments.

## Data visualization & dashboard design
- **Show Me the Numbers** — Stephen Few (2004/2012). The practical chart-selection guide: which chart for which data shape, and why most chart-library defaults are wrong for the data being shown.
- **Information Dashboard Design** — Stephen Few (2006/2013). Written for exactly this product category. Core rule: a dashboard is glanceable, not a report — if it needs scrolling or explaining, it's a report. Also covers why gauges, meters, and 3D charts usually fail on a real dashboard.
- **The Visual Display of Quantitative Information** — Edward Tufte (1983). The foundational text — data-ink ratio, removing "chartjunk," maximizing information shown per pixel.
- **The Truthful Art** — Alberto Cairo (2016). A framework for whether a visualization is functional, truthful, and actually insightful, not just attractive.
- **How Charts Lie** — Alberto Cairo (2019). How visualizations mislead, intentionally or not — worth reading since users will trust whatever the charts show them.
- **Storytelling with Data** — Cole Nussbaumer Knaflic (2015). The most widely used practical industry guide for framing a chart around the one thing it should communicate.
- **Lean Analytics** — Alistair Croll & Benjamin Yoskovitz (2013). How to choose metrics that mean something over vanity metrics that just go up — directly relevant to the S9 custom-formula engine.
- **Site Reliability Engineering** (Google, free online). The standard reference on alert design: alert on what a human must act on, not on everything that changed. Relevant once notifications get built — noted for later, not yet in the session plan.

*Applied — four rules that cover most of the actual design work:*
1. Chart follows data shape, not preference: comparison → bar, trend → line, part-of-whole → stacked bar, distribution → histogram. Skip pie charts — position/length judgments are measurably more accurate than angle judgments (Cleveland & McGill, above), not just a style call.
2. Every metric gets a one-sentence written definition before it gets a chart — if you can't state what action a number should drive, it's a vanity metric.
3. A dashboard answers a question at a glance. Fewer numbers, bigger ones, beats comprehensive-but-busy.
4. Notifications fire on what needs action, not on everything that changed.

## Direct application map
| Principle | Product feature |
|---|---|
| Ability > Motivation (Fogg) | One-tap/auto-sync connectors, no manual data entry where avoidable |
| Investment loop (Eyal, ethical version) | Dashboards get visibly richer/more useful the longer you use them |
| Autonomy (SDT) | User picks metrics/layout, not a forced template |
| Competence (SDT) | Visible progress states, trend indicators |
| Peak-end rule (Kahneman) | Satisfying micro-animation on data refresh; clean session close |
| Zeigarnik effect | Setup progress bar ("3 of 5 sources connected") |
| IKEA effect | Let new users customize at least one thing before first real use |

## UI craft & perceived speed
- **Refactoring UI** — Adam Wathan & Steve Schoger (2018). The most practical, dev-friendly book on why interfaces look polished: spacing, contrast, hierarchy, with before/after examples. Read this first if you're building the UI yourself.
- **Designing Interfaces** — Jenifer Tidwell. A pattern catalog — when to use which UI pattern and why, grounded in how people actually scan and act on screens.
- Response-time thresholds (Nielsen, building on Miller 1968): under 0.1s feels instant, under 1s feels continuous (no loading indicator needed), over 1s attention starts to drift, over 10s people give up unless progress is shown. *Applied:* anything under 1s needs zero loading UI; anything longer needs a skeleton or progress state.
- Optimistic UI — the actual technique behind tools that "feel" instant: update the screen immediately on user action, sync to the server after, roll back only on failure. Does more for perceived speed than any backend optimization. *Applied directly to S6:* a block moves the instant you drag it; the save happens invisibly after.
- Skeleton screens beat spinners for identical actual load time, measured repeatedly in UX research — use skeletons for connector/chart loading states, not spinners.

## Design identity & systems (why some products feel unmistakably themselves)
- **Design Systems** — Alla Kholmatova (2017). How consistent identity actually gets built: not a logo, a rule set (spacing scale, type scale, motion timing, color roles) applied everywhere without exception.
- **Apple's Human Interface Guidelines** — apple.com/design. Apple's own public design philosophy (clarity, deference, depth). Worth reading directly since it's the source, not someone's interpretation of it.
- **Universal Principles of Design** — Lidwell, Holden & Butler. About 125 design principles (alignment, hierarchy, affordance, consistency), each with a visual example — a reference to check any screen against.
- The honest mechanism behind "why does Notion/Apple feel like itself": it's not a special gift, it's a small rule set — one spacing scale, two or three font sizes, one accent color, one motion curve — applied with zero exceptions across every screen. Inconsistency, not lack of creativity, is what makes most products feel generic. *Applied:* before S3/S4, write down your own tiny rule set and don't deviate from it once building starts.
